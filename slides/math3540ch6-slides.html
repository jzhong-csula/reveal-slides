Chapter 6 Bonds

Introduction

Terminology

  • A bond is a security, issued by a government entity or corporation, that promises certain payments at future dates.
  • The time of purchase of the bond is called the issue date.
  • The life of a bond is finite; there is a maturity date (or redemption date) when the last payment occurs.
  • If the maturity date is fixed, the bond is said to be noncallable. But there may be a stipulation that the bond may be called earlier and the redemption amount must be given to the holder of the bond, and such bonds are referred to as callable bonds.
  • The term of the bond is the interval between the issue date and the maturity date (or the length of that interval, depending on the context).

More Vocabulary

  • Zero-coupon bonds (or pure discount bonds) have only a single payment at the fixed maturity date.
  • The bonds that have intermediate payments are called coupon bonds.
  • The time periods between two consecutive payments that occur before maturity are referred to as coupon periods.
  • The indenture is the legal document (contract) that specifies the terms and conditions of a particular.

Bond Alphabet Soup and the Basic Price Formula

What’s printed on the bond?

  • Let \(F\) be the face (or par) value of a bond.
  • The par value is used to calculate the size of the coupon payments.
  • Assume that \(m\) is the number of coupons issued during a year and that \(\alpha\) is the nominal rate convertible \(m\) times per year; then, \(r = \alpha/m\) is the effective rate per coupon period.
  • Then, the amount of a coupon payment equals \(\displaystyle Fr = \frac{F \alpha}{m}\).
  • Caveat: Both \(\alpha\) and \(r\) can be referred to as coupon rates.

Modified Coupon Rate

  • If \(n\) denotes the number of coupon periods in the term of the bond, and the bond is an \(N\text{-year}\) bond, then \(Nm = n\).
  • The redemption amount is the amount that the bond holder receives at the bond’s maturity date, excluding the regular coupon amount, denoted as \(C\).
  • If \(F = C\), then the bond is called a par-value bond (or redeemable at par).
  • Convention: If it is not specified otherwise, one should assume that \(F=C\).
  • The modified coupon rate \(g\) is defined by \(\displaystyle g := \frac{Fr}{C}\).

Investor’s Effective Yield Rate

Here we reserve the letter \(i\) for an annual effective yield rate, and use \(j\) for the investor’s effective yield rate per coupon period.

We also define the discount factor per coupon period as \(\displaystyle v_j = \frac{1}{1 + j}\). Let \(\displaystyle G = \frac{Fr}{j}\), and call \(G\) the base amount.

Now, the coupon amount equals \(Fr = Cg = Gj\).

Basic Price Formula

Let \(P\) be the price paid for the bond by the investor if \(j\) is the desired yield rate per period.

Question How to compute \(P\)?

\begin{align*} P = (Fr)\cdot a_{\overline{n}|j} + C v_j^n = (Fr)\cdot a_{\overline{n}|j} + K, \end{align*}

where \(K = C v_j^n\) is the value of the redemption amount at the issue date (time 0).

The Premium Discount Formula

Formula Derivation

Recall that

\begin{align*} a_{\overline{n}|j} = \frac{1 - v_j^n}{j}, \quad \text{or equivalently,} \quad v_j^n = 1 - j a_{\overline{n}|j}, \end{align*}

and also the basic price formula, \(\displaystyle P = (Fr) a_{\overline{n}|j} + C v_j^n\).

Using the fact that \(Fr = Cg\), we have \(\displaystyle P = (Cg)a_{\overline{n}|j} + C(1- j a_{\overline{n}|j})\), and thus,

\begin{align*} P = C(g - j)a_{\overline{n}|j} + C, \end{align*}

which is known as the premium-discount pricing formula for a bond.

Sell at a Premium

If \(P > C\), we say that the bond sells at a premium.

The value \(P - C\) is called the premium or amount of premium for the bond, i.e.,

\begin{align*} P - C = C(g - j) a_{\overline{n}|j}. \end{align*}

It is obvious that a bond sells at a premium if and only if \(g>j\).

Sell at a Discount

If \(C > P\), we say that the bond sells at a discount.

The value \(C - P\) is called the discount or amount of discount for the bond, i.e.,

\begin{align*} C - P = C(j - g) a_{\overline{n}|j}. \end{align*}

It is obvious that a bond sells at a discount if and only if \(j > g\).

An Example

Find the price of a \(1,000\) par value \(10\text{-year}\) bond with coupons at \(8.4\%\) convertible semiannually, which will be redeemed at \(1,050\). The bond is bought to yield \(10\%\) convertible semiannually.

First of all, identify the parameters \(F, C, r, g, j, n, K\) and \(G\).

\(F = 1000, C = 1050\)

\(r = \frac{0.084}{2} = 0.042, g = \frac{1000}{1050}\cdot 0.042 = 0.04\)

\(j = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05, n = 20\)

\(K = 1050 \cdot 1.05^{-20} = 395.7340\)

\(G = \frac{0.042}{0.05}\cdot 1000 = 840\).

An Example - Basic Pricing Formula

\begin{align*} P & = F r a_{\overline{n}|} + K\\ & = 1000\cdot 0.042 \cdot a_{\overline{20}|0.05} + 395.7340\\ & = 42 \cdot 12.4622 + 395.7340\\ & = 919.15 \end{align*}

An Example - Premium-Discount Pricing Formula

\begin{align*} P & = C(g - j)a_{\overline{n}|j} + C\\ & = 1050(0.04 - 0.05) \cdot a_{\overline{20}|0.05} + 1050\\ & = -10.5\cdot 12.4622 + 1050\\ & = 919.15 \end{align*}

Example 6.3.6

A \(7.5\text{-year}\) \(14\%\) bond with a face value of \(2,500\) has semiannual coupons and is sold to yield \(7.2\%\) convertible semiannually. The discount on the bond is \(283.12\). Find the price of the bond.

Solution

Let \(D = 283.12\) be the amount of discount. Then we have

\begin{align*} P = F r a_{\overline{n}|j} + C v_j^n, \quad \text{and}\quad D = C - P. \end{align*}

Use substitution, we obtain \(\displaystyle P = \frac{F r a_{\overline{n}|j} + D v_j^n}{1 - v_j^n}\).

Since \(F = 2500, N = 7.5, m = 2, n = Nm = 15, \alpha = 0.14, r = \alpha/m, j = 0.072/2\), and \(v_j = 1/(1 + j)\), we finally have \(P = 5265.69\).

Base Amount and Makeham’s Formula

Base Amount Formula

Recall that \(\displaystyle a_{\overline{n}|j} = \frac{1 - v^n_j}{j}\), and \(\displaystyle P = Fr \left( \frac{1 - v_j^n}{j} \right) + Cv_j^n\).

We replace the coupon amount \(F r\) by the alternative expression \(Gj\), then

\begin{align*} P = G - Gv_j^n + Cv_j^n = (C - G)v_j^n + G \end{align*}

which is called the base amount formula.

Interpretation of \(G\): since \(\displaystyle G = \frac{Cg}{j}\), we can consider \(G\) as the present value of a perpetuity paying \(Cg\) forever at interest rate \(j\).

Example 6.4.6

An eight-year bond has annual coupons and a redemption value of \(2,338\). It is purchased to yield \(9\%\) and each coupon is for \(63\). Use the base amount formula to calculate the price of the bond.

Solution

  • \(n = 8, j = 0.09, C = 2338\) and \(Fr = Cg = Gj =63\).
  • \(\displaystyle G = \frac{63}{0.09} = 700\).
  • \(P = (2338 - 700)(1.09)^{-8} + 700 = 1522.06\).

Makeham’s Formula

Recall again \(\displaystyle P = Fr \left( \frac{1 - v_j^n}{j} \right) + Cv_j^n\).

If we substitute the product \(Cg\) for \(Fr\) in the equation above, we find

\begin{align*} P = Cg \left( \frac{1 - v_j^n}{j} \right) + Cv_j^n = \frac{g}{j}C(1 - v_j^n) + Cv_j^n. \end{align*}

Note that \(K = Cv_j^n\), thus we have the Makeham’s formula:

\begin{align*} P = \frac{g}{j}(C - K) + K. \end{align*}

This formula is useful when the present value \(K\) of the redemption amount is known but the number of coupons \(n\) has not be given.

Example 6.4.5

A \(2,000\) bond with coupon rate of \(10\%\) payable quarterly is redeemable after an unspecified number of periods for \(2,250\). The bond is bought to yield \(8\%\) convertible quarterly. If the present value of the redemption is \(869.71\), find the purchase price.

Solution using Makeham’s formula

  • \(F = 2000, m = 4, \alpha=10\%, C = 2250, j = 0.08/4 = 0.02, K = 869.71\).
  • What else we need to make use of the Makeham’s formula?
  • \(g = Fr/C = 2000\cdot (0.1/4) / 2250 = 1/45\).
  • \(\displaystyle P = \frac{1/45}{0.02}(2250 - 869.71) + 869.71 = 2403.37\).

Bond Amortization Schedules

Bonds as Loans

  • The bonds can be seen as loans that the holder of the bond gives to the issuer of the bond; the coupon payments and the redemption payment are there to repay this loan.
  • The coupon period plays the role of the payment period we are familiar with from the context of amortized loans.
  • The investor’s effective yield rate per coupon period \(j\) stands for the per payment period interest rate \(i\) in the set-up for loan repayment.
  • The above analogy justifies the construction of amortization tables for bonds.

Notations

  • \(B_t\) denotes the balance of debt at time \(t\), immediately after any time \(t\) coupon payment, but before redemption payment, and commonly called the book value at time \(t\).
  • Caveat: \(B_t\) is calculated using the investor’s yield rate \(j\) and does not take into account market forces.
  • In particular: \(B_0 = P, B_n = C\).
  • The interest due at the time of the \(t\text{-th}\) coupon: \(I_t = jB_{t-1}\).
  • The amount of adjustment of principal in the \(t\text{-th}\) coupon: \(P_t = B_t - B_{t-1}\).

How to find \(B_t\)?

From the basic price formula:

\begin{align*} B_t = F r a_{\overline{n-t}|j} + Cv_j^{n-t}, \quad t = 0, 1, 2,\dots, n. \end{align*}

From the premium-discount formula:

\begin{align*} B_t = C(g - j) a_{\overline{n-t}|j} + C, \quad t = 0, 1, 2,\dots, n. \end{align*}

Therefore,

\begin{align*} I_t &= jB_{t - 1} = Cg - C(g - j)v_j^{n-t+1}\\ P_t &= B_{t - 1} - B_t = C(g - j)v_j^{n- t +1}. \end{align*}

Hence, \(I_t + P_t = Cg\), i.e., the coupons consist of the payment of interest due and the repayment of principal.

Premium vs Discount

At a premium:

If \(g>j\), i.e., a bond sells at a premium, then \(P_t\) is positive for every \(t\). So, \(P_t\) is a portion of the coupon which compensates the investor for the premium initially paid for the bond and the rest goes towards the interest payment on that premium.

At a discount:

If \(j>g\), i.e., a bond sells at a discount, then \(P_t\) is negative for every \(t\). Thus, not even the interest due on the balance can be covered by the coupon payments meaning that the remainder of the interest due is added onto the outstanding loan balance which increases as time goes by.

\(P=C\): \(P_t = 0\) for all \(t\).

Amount for Amortization of Premium

  • If the bond is sold at a premium (\(g>j\)), we usually refer to \(P_t\) as the amount for amortization of premium.
  • In this case, the book values \(B_t\) decrease as \(t\) increases (see Figure 6.5.9).
  • The process of find the values of \(B_t\) is called writing down the bond.
  • Also, \(P_t=C(g - j)v_j^{n- t +1}\) form an increasing sequence in \(t\).

Amount for Accumulation of Discount

  • If the bond is sold at a discount (\(j>g\)), we usually refer to \(-P_t\) as the amount for amortization of discount.
  • In this case, the book values \(B_t\) increase as \(t\) increases (see Figure 6.5.10).
  • The process of find the values of \(B_t\) is called writing up the bond.
  • Also, \(-P_t=C(j - g)v_j^{n- t +1}\) form an increasing sequence in \(t\).

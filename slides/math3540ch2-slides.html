Chapter 2 Equations of value and yield rates

Motivation

A financial transaction may be as simple as making a single deposit, or more commonly, there is a series of deposits and withdrawals before one focuses on the accumulation.

For example, you would like to save money each month to buy a house, or at least to have the down payment.

You might have a predictable contribution/expense schedule, the question is when you can buy the house and become a homeowner.

This is why we study the equations of value in this chapter.

Single deposit under compound interest

The amount function

Let the principal be denoted by \(C\). Then, in the usual notation, the amount function with the compound interest \(i\) reads as

\begin{align*} A_C(T)= C(1 + i)^T\qquad \text{for every } T \ge 0. \end{align*}

The above equation is referred to as the \(T\) time equation of value.

Knowing any three among the values \(A_C(T), C, T\) and \(i\) allows us to calculate the fourth one.

We will illustrate this fact through examples.

Unknown principal

Assume that the annual effective compound interest rate is \(6.25\%\).

How much would you have to deposit on October 1, 2020 in order to be able to withdraw the \(1,000\) from the account on July 1, 2024?

Unknown principal - Solution

The start of the loan term is October 1, 2020. So we call that time zero. Hence, the time at which we are given the accumulated amount we want to produce is

\begin{align*} T = 3.75\quad \text{(in years)}. \end{align*}

The equation of value reads as

\begin{align*} 1000 = C (1 + 0.0625)^{3.75}, \end{align*}

so

\begin{align*} C = \frac{1000}{(1 + 0.0625)^{3.75}} = 796.65. \end{align*}

Unknown length of investment

Find the length of time necessary for \(1,000\) to accumulate to \(1,500\) if invested at \(6\%\) per annum compounded semi-annually.

Solution

It is more convenient to think of time-units in this situation as half-years.

Let us denote the unknown number of half-years by \(n\).

Then the equation of value reads as

\begin{align*} 1000 \left( 1 + \frac{0.06}{2} \right)^n = 1500. \end{align*}

So \(\displaystyle n = \frac{\ln(1500/1000)}{\ln(1 + 0.06/2)} = 13.7\),

or, the sought for the length of time is about \(6.85\) years.

Unknown time - Doubling your money

At a nominal interest rate of \(10\%\) convertible quarterly, how long will it take you to double your money?

Solution

Since the wealth accumulated at any given time is proportional to the principal, we can assume (without loss of generality) that the initial investment is \(1\).

Then the equation of value becomes

\begin{align*} \left( 1 + \frac{0.1}{4} \right)^n = 2, \end{align*}

where \(n\) denotes the unknown length of time (in quarter-years) that it takes to double the initial investment.

Unknown time - Doubling your money - Continued

Solving for \(n\):

\begin{align*} n = \frac{\ln 2}{\ln ( 1 + 0.1/4 )} = 28.071, \end{align*}

and so the length of time we looking for is about \(7\) years.

Unknown interest rate

At what interest rate convertible quarterly would \(1,000\) accumulate to \(1,600\) in six years?

Solution

We need to find \(i^{(4)}\) from the equation of value

\begin{align*} 1000 \left( 1 + \frac{i^{(4)}}{4} \right)^{24} = 1600. \end{align*}

We get

\begin{align*} \frac{i^{(4)}}{4} = (1.6)^{1/24} - 1 = 0.019776, \end{align*}

and so \(i^{(4)} = 0.0791\).

Investment with multiple contributions

The time \(\tau\) equation of value

Generally, we may have a sequence of contributions \(\{C_{t_k}\}\).

A negative contribution \(C_{t_k}\) means there is a withdrawal at time \(t_k\)

This This sequence of investments is liquidated at a time \(T\) and the resulting balance is denoted by \(B\).

Suppose that a certain intermediate time \(\tau\) bears a significance for the investor and that the investor wants to value all the contributions at that specific time \(\tau\).

The time \(\tau\) equation of value - Continued

Then the value of all the combined contributions to the account valued at time \(\tau\) must be equal to the final balance B valued at time \(\tau\).

Formally, we have the time \(\tau\) equation of value:

\begin{align*} \sum_k C_{t_k} \frac{a(\tau)}{a(t_k)} = B \frac{a(\tau)}{a(T)}. \end{align*}

Why?

Important Fact 1.7.5 on page 31.

The time \(0\) equation of value

In the case that we choose \(\tau =0\), the time \(0\) equation of value becomes

\begin{align*} \sum_k C_{t_k} v(t_k) = B v(T). \end{align*}

In words, it suffices to discount all the contributions and the balance from the times their corresponding cash flows took place to the initial time \(0\).

The time \(T\) equation of value

In the case that we set \(\tau = T\), we have as the time \(T\) equation of value

\begin{align*} \sum_k C_{t_k} \frac{a(T)}{a(t_k)} = B. \end{align*}

That is, all the contributions must be augmented to their value at the final time \(T\).

An example: Unknown payment

In return for a promise to receive \(600\) at the end of \(8\) years, Roger agrees to pay \(100\) now, \(200\) at the end of \(5\) years, and to make a further payment \(X\) at the end of \(10\) years.

Assume that the nominal rate of interest is \(8\%\) convertible semi-annually.

Find \(X\).

An example: Unknown payment - Solution

We can assume here \(T = 10\), and \(B = 0\). So we are looking for a payment that is made at time \(\tau = 10\).

Then the equation of value is

\begin{align*} 100(1.04)^{20} + 200(1 + 1.04)^{10} - 600(1.04)^4 + X = 0, \end{align*}

which give

\begin{align*} X = 186.76. \end{align*}

An example: Unknown rate of interest

At what effective rate of interest will the present value of the investment consisting of \(2,000\) deposited at the end of \(2\) years and \(3,000\) deposited at the end of four years be equal to \(4,000\)?

An example: Unknown rate of interest - Solution

The time \(0\) equation of value is

\begin{align*} 2000 v^2 + 3000 v^4 = 4000, \end{align*}

where \(v = (1 + i)^{-1}\) is the discount factor.

We obtain a quadratic equation in \(v^2\):

\begin{align*} 3 v^4 + 2v^2 - 4 = 0, \end{align*}

which yields only one positive solution \(v^2 = 0.8685\).

So, \(i = \frac{1}{v} - 1 = 0.073\).

An example: Unknown payment times

The present value of two payments of \(100\) each to be made at the end of \(n\) years and \(2n\) year is \(100\). If \(i = 0.08\), find \(n\).

An example: Unknown payment times - Solution

The time \(0\) equation of value:

\begin{align*} 100(1+0.08)^{-n} + 100(1+1.08)^{-2n} = 100. \end{align*}

Setting \(A = (1.08)^{-n}\), we have

\begin{align*} A^2 + A - 1 = 0, \end{align*}

which has only one positive solution \(\displaystyle A = \frac{\sqrt{5} -1}{2}\).

So

\begin{align*} n = - \frac{\ln(A)}{\ln(1.08)} = 6.25. \end{align*}

Investment return

An example: Unknown rate

At an unknown interest rate \(i\), an investment of \(1,000\) immediately and \(1,500\) at the end of the second year accumulates to \(2,600\) at the end of the fourth year. Find \(i\).

Solution

Setting \(j = 1 + i\), then the equation of value at time \(\tau =4\) reads as

\begin{align*} 1000 j^4 + 1500 j^2 = 2600. \end{align*}

Solving the quadratic equation, we get \(j^2 = 1.028342\). So \(i = 0.014\).

Note: The rate we have just calculated is known at the yield rate for an investment.

Yield rate

Formally, recall the equation of value with reference time \(\tau\), i.e.,

\begin{align*} \sum_k C_{t_k} (1 + i)^{\tau - t_k} = B(1 + i)^{\tau - T}. \end{align*}

The rate of interest \(i\) which satisfies the above equation (for all other ingredients given and fixed) is called the (annual) yield rate or internal rate of return for that investment.

The yield rate is often understood as a measure of quality of a certain investment.

There is slightly problem with this line of thinking, as we can see from the following examples …

An example: Non-unique yield rate

Consider a transaction in which Gautam makes payments of \(100\) immediately and \(132\) at the end of the two years in exchange for a payment in return of \(230\) at the end of one year. What is the yield rate of this investment scheme?

An example: Non-unique yield rate - Solution

The equation of value is:

\begin{align*} 100(1+i)^2 + 132 = 230(1+i), \end{align*}

which gives the quadratic equation

\begin{align*} (1 + i)^2 - 2.3(1 + i) + 1.32 = 0. \end{align*}

Now, we factor and obtain

\begin{align*} [(1+i) - 1.1]\cdot [(1+i) - 1.2] = 0, \end{align*}

so that both \(i = 0.1\) and \(i=0.2\) may be considered as solutions!

An example: Undefined yield

Assume that Johnson is able to borrow \(100\) from a bank for one year at \(8\%\) effective and immediately loan out \(100\) to Harry for one year at \(10\%\) effective. What is Johnson’s yield rate for this combination of transaction?

An example: Undefined yield - Solution

Apparently, Johnson is able to make a \(2\) profit at the end the one year - without any net investment at time zero.

We might say that this means that the yield rate is infinite …

However, if Johnson were able to borrow \(1000\) and then lend that amount of money to Harry, his profit at the end of th one year would be \(20\); this is clearly preferable to the \(2\) profit.

It is sensible to wish to be able to compare the above two cases, but this comparison is not allowed through the definition of the yield rate …

A sufficient condition for uniqueness of yield rates

Hopefully, we have managed to illustrate the importance of showing the existence and uniqueness of yield rates …

An easily verified condition (Important Fact 2.4.9) for uniqueness of yield rates is the following:

Suppose that the contributions take place at times \(t_1 < t_2 <\cdots < t_n\) and that there exists a yield rate \(i>-1\) such that the outstanding balances

\begin{align*} B_{t_k} = \sum_{q=1}^k C_{t_q}(1 + i)^{t_k-t_q}, \quad \text{for } k \le n-1 \end{align*}

are all of the same sign. Then \(i\) is the unique yield rate greater than \(-1\).

Example 2.4.7 - Three party example

Brian, Filemon, and Harold are friends. Brian will pay Filemon \(1,000\) now. Filemon will pay Brian \(300\) and Harold \(800\) in exactly one year. Finally, Harold will pay Brian \(900\) two years from now. What is Brian’s annual yield for this three-way transaction spanning two years?

Example 2.4.7 - Three party example - Solution

We only focus on Brian’s investment, and the time \(2\) equation of value is

\begin{align*} 1000(1 + i)^2 - 300(1 + i) - 900 = 0. \end{align*}

The quadratic equation then tell us that

\begin{align*} 1 + i = \frac{3 \pm \sqrt{9 + 360}}{20}. \end{align*}

Note that Brian invests \(1,000\) and receives total \(1,200 > 1,000\), he has a positive yield rate, namely,

\begin{align*} i = \frac{3 + \sqrt{9 + 360}}{20} - 1 = 0.11046836. \end{align*}

Bottom line approach

When computing Brian’s yield rate in Example (2.4.7), we were not concerned with the source of the money coming in or the recipient of outgoing money.

Rather, we took a “bottom line approach.” We were only concerned with the times and amounts of all contributions (positive or negative) by Brian. This approach is fundamental to the successful determination of yield rates.

Reinvestment

Example 2.5.1

Jose loans Martin \(12,000\). Martin repays the loan by paying \(5,000\) at the end of two years and \(10,000\) at the end of four years. The money received at \(t = 2\) is immediately reinvested at an annual effective interest rate of \(2.4\%\).

Find Martin’s rate of interest and Jose’s annual yield rate.

Example 2.5.1 - Solution

For Martin, the time \(4\) equation of value is

\begin{align*} 12000(1 + i)^4 - 5000(1 + i)^2 - 10000 = 0, \end{align*}

which gives \(i = 0.69894953\).

For Jose,

\begin{align*} 12000(1 + i_J)^4 = 5000(1 + 0.024)^2 + 10000, \end{align*}

which gives \(i_J = 0.061625755\).

Note: Jose’s yield rate is lower than Martin’s interest since Jose reinvested the $5,000 at an interest rate lower than Martin’s rate.

Chapter 2 Vector Functions of a Single Variable

2.1 Differentiation

  • A vector-valued function \(\mathbf{F}\) of a single variable is a rule that associate a vector \(\mathbf{F}(t)\) to each real number \(t\) in some set, usually an interval \(t \in [t_1, t_2]\) or a collection of intervals.
  • Example: \(\mathbf{F}(t) = (1/t) \mathbf{i}\) defined for \(t \neq 0\).
  • Limits
    • We say that a vector-valued function \(\mathbf{F}\) has a limit \(\mathbf{A}\) at \(t_0\) and write \[ \lim_{t\to t_0} \mathbf{F}(t) = \mathbf{A}, \quad\text{or}\quad \mathbf{F}(t) \to \mathbf{A}\, \text{as}~t \to t_0, \] if the real-valued function \(f(t) = |\mathbf{F}(t) - \mathbf{A}|\) has a limit \(0\) at \(t_0\), i.e., \[ \lim_{t \to t_0} |\mathbf{F}(t) - \mathbf{A}| = 0, \] which means that for any \(\varepsilon >0\), there is \(\delta >0\) such that \[ \vert \mathbf{F}(t) - \mathbf{A} \vert < \varepsilon \quad \text{whenever}\quad | t - t_0 | < \delta. \]
  • Intuitively, this means that the magnitude and the direction of the vector \(\mathbf{F}(t)\) approaches the magnitude and direction of the vector \(\mathbf{A}\).
    • Consequently, this also means that the components of the vector \(\mathbf{F}(t)\) approach the components of the vector \(\mathbf{A}\), i.e., \[ \lim_{t \to t_0} \mathbf{F}^i(t) = \mathbf{A}^i. \]
  • Continuity
    • We say that a vector-valued function \(\mathbf{F}\) is continuous at \(t_0\) if \[ \lim_{t\to t_0} \mathbf{F}(t) = \mathbf{F}(t_0). \]

    • This means that the components and the magnitude of the vector \(\mathbf{F}(t)\) are continuous at \(t_0\), i.e.,

      \begin{align*} \lim_{t \to t_0 } \mathbf{F}^i(t) & = \mathbf{F}^i(t_0),\\ \lim_{t \to t_0} |\mathbf{F}(t)| & = |\mathbf{F}(t_0)|. \end{align*}
  • Derivatives
    • We say that a vector-valued function \(\mathbf{F}\) is differentiable at \(t_0\) if the limit \[ \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\mathbf{F}(t_0 + \Delta t) - \mathbf{F}(t_0)}{\Delta t} \] exists; this limit is then called the derivative of \(\mathbf{F}(t)\) at \(t_0\) and is written \[ \mathbf{F}'(t_0)\quad\text{or}\quad \frac{d \mathbf{F}}{dt}(t_0). \]

    • Properties of derivatives

      \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt}(\mathbf{F}+ \mathbf{G}) & = \frac{d \mathbf{F}}{dt} + \frac{d \mathbf{G}}{dt} \label{eq:2.4}\tag{2.4}\\ \frac{d}{dt}(s \mathbf{F}) & = \frac{ds }{dt} \mathbf{F} + s \frac{d \mathbf{F}}{dt} \label{eq:2.5}\tag{2.5}\\ \frac{d}{dt}(\mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{G}) & = \frac{d \mathbf{F}}{dt} \cdot \mathbf{G} + \mathbf{F} \cdot \frac{d \mathbf{G}}{dt} \label{eq:2.6}\tag{2.6}\\ \frac{d}{dt}(\mathbf{F} \times \mathbf{G}) & = \frac{d \mathbf{F}}{dt} \times \mathbf{G} + \mathbf{F} \times \frac{d \mathbf{G}}{dt} \label{eq:2.7}\tag{2.7}\\ \end{align*}

    • Proof of Eq. \eqref{eq:2.7}: With the definition of the derivative in mind, we write

      \begin{align*} & \frac{\mathbf{F}(t + \Delta t) \times \mathbf{G}(t + \Delta t) - \mathbf{F}(t) \times \mathbf{G}(t)}{\Delta t}\\ =\, & \frac{[\mathbf{F}(t + \Delta t) - \mathbf{F}(t)] \times \mathbf{G}(t+\Delta t)}{\Delta t} + \frac{\mathbf{F}(t) \times [\mathbf{G}(t + \Delta t) - \mathbf{G}(t)]}{\Delta t}. \end{align*}

      As \(\Delta t \to 0\), the right-hand side approaches a limiting value given by \[ \frac{d \mathbf{F}}{dt} \times \mathbf{G} + \mathbf{F} \times \frac{d \mathbf{G}}{dt}, \] and the limit of the left-hand side is nothing but \[ \frac{d}{dt}(\mathbf{F} \times \mathbf{G}). \]

    • From the definition and properties of derivatives, vector differentiation is like scalar differentiation, treating \(\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}\) and \(\mathbf{k}\) as constants. If \[ \mathbf{F}(t) = P(t) \mathbf{i} + Q(t) \mathbf{j} + R(t) \mathbf{k}, \] then \[ \mathbf{F}'(t) = P'(t) \mathbf{i} + Q'(t) \mathbf{j} + R'(t) \mathbf{k}. \]
  • Examples Find the derivative of the following vector-valued functions.
    • \(\mathbf{F}(t) = t^3 \mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}\).
    • \(\mathbf{F}(t) = \sin t \mathbf{i} + \cos t \mathbf{j} + t \mathbf{k}\).
    • \(\mathbf{F}(t) = \mathbf{i} + 2 \mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}\).

  • Example 2.7 Prove that, if \(\mathbf{F}(t)\) has constant nonzero magnitude (varies only in direction), then \(\mathbf{F}'(t)\) is either the zero vector or it is a nonzero vector perpendicular to \(\mathbf{F}(t)\).

    Solution

    • If \(|\mathbf{F}(t)|\) is a constant, then \(\mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{F}\) is also a constant.
    • Thus, the derivative of \(\mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{F}\) should be zero.
    • But using Eq. \eqref{eq:2.6}, we have \[ \frac{d \mathbf{F}}{dt} \cdot \mathbf{F} + \mathbf{F} \cdot \frac{d \mathbf{F}}{dt} = 2 \mathbf{F} \cdot \frac{d \mathbf{F}}{dt}. \]
    • Therefore, we have \[ \mathbf{F} \cdot \frac{d \mathbf{F}}{dt} = 0. \]

2.2 Space Curve, Velocities, and Tangents

  • Recall that we represent a line in vector form: \[ \mathbf{R}(t) = \mathbf{R}_0 + t \mathbf{V}. \]
  • In this section, we consider parametric equations of the form \[ \mathbf{R} = \mathbf{R}(t), \] where \(\mathbf{R}(t)\) is a (continuous) vector-valued function called a space curve or an arc.

  • Note: Different functions may parameterize the same curve. For example,

    \begin{align*} \mathbf{R}_1(t) & = \mathbf{R}_0 + t \mathbf{W}, && t \in \mathbb{R}\\ \mathbf{R}_2(t) & = \mathbf{R}_0 + \tan t \mathbf{W}, && t \in (-\pi/2, \pi/2). \end{align*}
  • Circle
    • Parametric equation of the circle \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\) in the \(z = 0\) plane: \[ \mathbf{R}(t) = \cos t \mathbf{i} + \sin t \mathbf{j}. \]

    • Parametric equation of the circle centered at \(\mathbf{R}_0 = x_0 \mathbf{i} + y_0 \mathbf{j} + z_0 \mathbf{k}\), with radius \(\rho\):

      \begin{align*} x & = \rho \cos t + x_0,\\ y & = \rho \sin t + y_0,\\ z & = z_0, \end{align*}

      or simply, \[ \mathbf{R}(t) = \mathbf{R}_0 + \rho \cos t \mathbf{i} + \rho \sin t \mathbf{j}. \]

    • Parametric equation of the circle centered at \(\mathbf{R}_0\) with radius \(\rho\) lying in the plane spanned by the orthonormal vectors \((\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2)\):

      \begin{equation} \label{eq:2.13} \tag{2.13} \mathbf{R}(t) = \mathbf{R}_0 + \rho \cos t \mathbf{e}_1 + \rho \sin t \mathbf{e}_2. \end{equation}

  • Example 2.8 Parametrize the circle of radius \(2\), parallel to the \(xz\) plane, centered at \((0, 1, 0)\).

    Solution We use \(\mathbf{R}_0 = \mathbf{j}, \mathbf{e}_1 = \mathbf{i}\) and \(\mathbf{e}_2 = \mathbf{k}\) in Eq. \eqref{eq:2.13} to derive \[ \mathbf{R}(t) = \mathbf{j} + 2 \cos t \mathbf{i} + 2 \sin t \mathbf{k}. \]

  • Example 2.9 Parametrize the circle cutting the axes at the points \(x=1, y=1\), and \(z=1\) respectively.

    Solution

    • The circle should lie in the plane \(x + y + z = 1\).
    • The midpoint of the equilateral triangle formed by \(\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}\) and \(\mathbf{k}\) is the center of the circle, which is given by \[ \frac{1}{3} (\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}). \]
    • The radius of the circle is the distance from the midpoint to, say, \(\mathbf{i}\): \[ \rho = \left|\mathbf{i} - \frac{1}{3}(\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}) \right| = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{3}. \]
    • Two orthonormal vectors on the plane: \[ \mathbf{e}_1 = \frac{\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j}}{\sqrt{2}}, \quad \mathbf{e}_2 = \frac{\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} - 2 \mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{6}}. \]

    • By Eq. \eqref{eq:2.13}, we have

      \begin{align*} \mathbf{R}(t) & = \frac{1}{3}(\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}) + \frac{\sqrt{6}}{3} \cos t \frac{(\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j})}{\sqrt{2}} + \frac{\sqrt{6}}{3} \sin t \frac{(\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} - 2 \mathbf{k})}{\sqrt{6}}\\ & = \frac{1}{3}(\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}) + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3} \cos t (\mathbf{i} - \mathbf{j}) + \frac{1}{3} \sin t (\mathbf{i} + \mathbf{j} - 2 \mathbf{k}). \end{align*}

  • Let \((\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2, \mathbf{e}_3)\) be a right-handed orthonormal system. The helix of radius \(\rho\) and pitch \(2 \pi |a|\) with axis passing through \(\mathbf{R}_0\) and parallel to \(\mathbf{e}_3\) is described by

    \begin{equation} \label{eq:2.15} \tag{2.15} \mathbf{R}(t) = \mathbf{R}_0 + \rho \cos t \mathbf{e}_1 + \rho \sin t \mathbf{e}_2 + a t \mathbf{e}_3. \end{equation}

    The helix is right-handed for \(a > 0\) and left-handed for \(a<0\).

  • Example 2.10 Describe the helix generated by \(x = \cos t, y = 2t, z = \sin t\).

    Solution

    • This has the form of Eq. \eqref{eq:2.15}, with \(\mathbf{R}_0 = \mathbf{0}\), radius \(\rho = 1\), \(\mathbf{e}_1 = \mathbf{i}\), and \(\mathbf{e}_2 = \mathbf{k}\).
    • Note: \(\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}, \mathbf{k}\) is not a right-handed system, so we must take \(\mathbf{e}_3 = - \mathbf{j}\).
    • The parametric equation of the helix is \[ \mathbf{R}(t) = \cos t \mathbf{i} + \sin t \mathbf{k} - 2t \mathbf{j}. \]
    • This helix is left-handed and has pitch \(4 \pi\).

  • Example 2.11 Describe the helix of example 2.10 using nonparametric equations.

    Solution We can eliminate \(t\) in favor of \(y\) using \(t = y/2\). This results in \[ x = \cos (y/2), \quad z = \sin (y/2) \] as a nonparametric form.

  • If the space curve \(\mathbf{R}(t)\) is differentiable, the (instantaneous) velocity \(\mathbf{v}\) is defined as the derivative of \(\mathbf{R}(t)\), i.e. \[ \mathbf{v}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \mathbf{R}}{\Delta t} = \mathbf{R}'(t). \] The magnitude \(|\mathbf{v}|\) of \(\mathbf{v}\) is called the speed, denoted by \(\nu\).
  • Note:
    • The velocity vector \(\mathbf{v} = d \mathbf{R}/ dt\) is tangent to the curve.

    • We use the letter \(\mathbf{T}\) to denote the unit tangent vector:

      \begin{equation} \label{eq:2.19} \tag{2.19} \mathbf{T} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{\nu} = \frac{(dx/dt) \mathbf{i} + (dy/dt) \mathbf{j} + (dz/dt) \mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{(dx/dt)^2 + (dy/dt)^2 + (dz/dt)^2}}. \end{equation}

  • Example 2.12 Determine the unit vector tangent to the circle \(x = \cos t, y = \sin t, z = 0\) at (a) \(t = 0\) (b) \(t = \pi/2\)

    Solution

    • From Fig. 2.8, it is obvious that (a) \(\mathbf{j}\), (b) \(-\mathbf{i}\).
    • We can also see this by Eq. \eqref{eq:2.19}: \[ \mathbf{T} = \frac{-\sin t \mathbf{i} + \cos t \mathbf{j}}{\sqrt{\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t}} = - \sin t \mathbf{i} + \cos t \mathbf{j}. \]
    • For (a), \(t = 0\), we have \[ \mathbf{T} = - \sin 0 \mathbf{i} + \cos 0 \mathbf{j} = \mathbf{j}. \] For (b), \(t = \pi/2\), we have \[ \mathbf{T} = - \sin (\pi/2) \mathbf{i} + \cos (\pi/2) \mathbf{j} = - \mathbf{i}. \]

  • Example 2.13 Find the unit vector tangent to the curve \(x = t, y = t^2, z = t^3\), at the point \((2, 4, 8)\).

    Solution

    • When \(t = 2\), we have the point \((2, 4, 8)\).
    • The unit tangent vector is \[ \mathbf{T} = \frac{\mathbf{i} + 2t \mathbf{j} + 3t^2 \mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{1 + 4t^2 + 9t^4}}. \]
    • With \(t = 2\), we have \[ \mathbf{T} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{161}}( \mathbf{i} + 4 \mathbf{j} + 12 \mathbf{k} ). \]
  • A curve is smooth if there is parametrization \(\mathbf{R}(t), t \in [t_1, t_2]\), satisfying
    1. the velocity is continuous;
    2. there are no self-crossings;
    3. the velocity is non-zero.
  • A curve is a closed loop if \(\mathbf{R}(t_1) = \mathbf{R}(t_2)\).
  • A curve is regular if it consists of a finite number of smooth arcs joined together without self-crossings.
  • A curve is oriented if a direction is specified along it, usually described by the direction of increasing parameter.
  • Arc length
    • Suppose \(C\) is a smooth space curve, which is parametrized by \(\mathbf{R}(t)\), for, say \(a \le t \le b\). The length of the smooth curve is given by \[ \int_a^b \left|\frac{d \mathbf{R}}{dt}\right|\, dt = \int_a^b \left[ \left( \frac{dx}{dt} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{dy}{dt} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{dz}{dt} \right)^2 \right]^{1/2}\, dt. \]
    • The expression of the arc length is sometimes written as \[ \int_C [(dx)^2 + (dy)^2 + (dz)^2]^{1/2}\quad \text{or} \quad \int_C |d \mathbf{R}|, \] which emphasizes that the arc length is independent of a particular parametrization.

  • Example 2.14 Find the arc length between \((0, 0, 1)\) and \((1, 0, 1)\) of the helix \(y = \sin (2\pi x), z = \cos (2 \pi x)\).

    Solution

    • First, note that if we set \(x = t / (2\pi)\), we can parametrized the helix as in Eq. \eqref{eq:2.15}.
    • Since here \(y\) and \(z\) are functions of \(x\), we can re-write \[ (dx)^2 + (dy)^2 + (dz)^2 = (dx)^2 + 4\pi^2 \cos^2 (2\pi x)\, (dx)^2 + 4\pi^2 \sin^2 (2\pi x)(dx)^2. \]
    • Thus, the arc length desired is \[ \int_0^1 (1 + 4 \pi^2)^{1/2}\, dx = (1 + 4\pi^2)^{1/2}. \]
  • Natural parametrization
    • We can use the arc length as a parameter of a space curve: \[ s = s(t) = \int_{t_1}^t \left| \frac{d \mathbf{R}}{d\tau}\right|\, d\tau \quad (t\ge t_1). \]
    • Why? By the fundamental theorem of calculus, we have \[ \frac{ds}{dt} = \left| \frac{d \mathbf{R}}{dt}\right| = |\mathbf{v}|. \]
    • In component form, this becomes \[ \frac{ds}{dt} = \left[ \left( \frac{dx}{dt} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{dy}{dt} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{dz}{dt} \right)^2 \right]^{1/2}. \]
    • Since the curve is smooth, the velocity is not zero, i.e, \(ds/dt \neq 0\), by the chain rule, \[ \frac{d \mathbf{R}}{ds} = \frac{d \mathbf{R}/dt}{ds/dt}. \]
    • Note:
      1. \(d \mathbf{R}/ds\) is tangent to the curve, since \(d \mathbf{R}/dt\) (or the velocity) is.
      2. Moreover, \(d \mathbf{R}/ds\) is a unit tangent vector, so \[ \mathbf{T} = \frac{d \mathbf{R}}{ds}. \]

  • Example 2.15 Reparametrize the curve
    (i) \(\displaystyle \mathbf{R}(t) = \frac{t^2}{2}\mathbf{i} + \frac{t^3}{3} \mathbf{k}, \quad (0 \le t \le 2)\)

    in terms of arc length.

    Solution

    • Choosing \(t_1 = 0\), we have \[ s = \int_0^t \left| \frac{d \mathbf{R}}{d\tau}\right|\, d\tau = \int_0^t (\tau^2 + \tau^4)^{1/2} \, d\tau = \frac{(t^2 + 1)^{3/2} - 1}{3}. \]
    • Solving the equation for \(t\), \[ t = [(3s + 1)^{2/3} - 1]^{1/2}. \]
    • The new parametrization is \[ \mathbf{R}(s) = \frac{(3s + 1)^{2/3} -1}{2} \mathbf{i} + \frac{[(3s + 1)^{2/3} - 1]^{3/2}}{3} \mathbf{k}. \]
    • Furthermore, we can compute \[ \frac{d \mathbf{R}}{ds} = (3s + 1)^{-1/2} \mathbf{i} + [(3s + 1)^{2/3} -1]^{1/2}(3s + 1)^{-1/3} \mathbf{k}, \] which is a unit tangent vector to the curve.

2.3 Acceleration and Curvature

  • Acceleration
    • The acceleration of a particle is defined to be the time rate of change of its velocity.
    • Acceleration is associated with a change in either the magnitude or the direction of the velocity, or both.
    • First, we suppose that the particle moves along a straight line, i.e., the direction of the velocity is constant. In this case, the magnitude of the acceleration is just the rate of change of speed: \[ \vert \mathbf{a} \vert = \frac{d}{dt} \vert \mathbf{v} \vert = \frac{d^2 s}{dt^2}, \] where \(s\) is the arc length along the curve.
    • On the other hand, let’s assume the particle moves at constant speed around a circle of radius \(\rho\), it is well known that it undergoes a “centripetal” acceleration of magnitude \[ \vert \mathbf{a}\vert = \frac{|\mathbf{v}|^2}{\rho} = \frac{1}{\rho} \left( \frac{ds}{dt} \right)^2, \] directed toward the center of the circle. This is due solely to the change of direction.

    • In general, the acceleration vector can be expressed as the linear combination of two orthonormal vectors, one giving the rate of change of speed and the other giving the instantaneous centripetal acceleration corresponding to a related circular trajectory:

      \begin{equation} \label{eq:2.30} \tag{2.30} \mathbf{a} = a_t \mathbf{T} + a_n \mathbf{N}, \end{equation}

      where \[ a_t = \frac{d^2 s}{d t^2}, \quad a_n = \kappa |\mathbf{v}|^2. \]

  • Curvature
    • We define the curvature \(\kappa\) as the rate at which the unit tangent vector turns, with respect to arc length along the curve: \[ \kappa = \left| \frac{d \mathbf{T}}{ds} \right|. \]
    • We can also write \[ \kappa = \frac{|d \mathbf{T}/ dt|}{ds /dt} = \frac{|d \mathbf{T}/ dt|}{|d \mathbf{R}/ dt|} = \frac{|d \mathbf{T}/ dt|}{\nu}. \]
    • What does this give for the curvature of a circle of radius \(\rho\)?
      • In Fig. 2.16, the arc length between \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) on the circle is \[ \Delta s = \rho \Delta \theta. \]
      • The change of magnitude of the unit tangent vector is \[ \vert \Delta \mathbf{T} \vert = \vert \mathbf{T}_2 - \mathbf{T}_1 \vert = \Delta \theta. \]
      • Thus, \[ \left| \frac{\Delta \mathbf{T}}{\Delta s}\right| \approx \frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta s} = \frac{1}{\rho}. \]
      • The curvature of a circle as we have defined it is the reciprocal of its radius: \[ \kappa = \frac{1}{\rho}. \]
  • Normals
    • \(d \mathbf{T}/dt = \mathbf{0}\) or \(d \mathbf{T}/dt \perp \mathbf{T}\) since \(|\mathbf{T}| = 1\) (see Example 2.7).
    • If \(d \mathbf{T}/dt \neq \mathbf{0}\), we define the unit vector \[ \mathbf{N} = \frac{d \mathbf{T}/ dt}{|d \mathbf{T}/dt|} \] to be the principal normal.
    • Rearranging terms, we obtain \[ \frac{d \mathbf{T}}{dt} = \left| \frac{d \mathbf{T}}{dt}\right| \mathbf{N} = \kappa \left| \frac{d \mathbf{R}}{dt}\right| \mathbf{N} = \kappa \nu \mathbf{N}. \]
    • In natural parametrization, we have \[ \mathbf{N} = \frac{d \mathbf{T}/ ds}{|d \mathbf{T}/ ds |}. \] Thus, \[ \frac{d \mathbf{T}}{ds} = \kappa \mathbf{N}. \] In other words, \(\mathbf{T}\) turns in the direction \(\mathbf{N}\), at a rate \(\kappa\) (with respect to arc length).

  • Orthonormal decomposition of acceleration

    \begin{align*} \mathbf{a} & = \frac{d \mathbf{v}}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(\nu \mathbf{T})\\ & = \frac{d \nu}{dt} \mathbf{T} + \nu \frac{d \mathbf{T}}{dt}\\ & = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2} \mathbf{T} + \kappa \nu^2 \mathbf{N}, \end{align*}

    which gives us the Eq. \eqref{eq:2.30}.

    Also, we by the pythagorean theorem,

    \begin{equation} \label{eq:2.42} \tag{2.42} \vert \mathbf{a} \vert^2 = a_t^2 + a^2_n. \end{equation}

  • Example 2.16 The position of a particle moving around the circle \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\) in the \(xy\) plane, with angular velocity \(\omega\), is \[ x = r \cos (\omega t), \quad y = r \sin (\omega t), \quad z = 0. \] Find the normal and tangential components of acceleration of the particle, and determine the curvature of the circle.

    Solution

    • \(\mathbf{R}(t) = r \cos (\omega t) \mathbf{i} + r \sin (\omega t) \mathbf{j}\).
    • \(d \mathbf{R}/ dt = - r \omega \sin(\omega t) \mathbf{i} + r \omega \cos(\omega t) \mathbf{j}\).
    • \(d^2 \mathbf{R}/dt^2 = - r \omega^2 \cos(\omega t) \mathbf{i} - r \omega^2 \sin(\omega t) \mathbf{j}\).
    • \(\nu = |d \mathbf{R}/dt| = (r^2 \omega^2 \sin^2(\omega t) + r^2 \omega^2 \cos^2(\omega t))^{1/2} = \omega r\).
    • \(a_t = d\nu/ dt = 0\).
    • \(|\mathbf{a}| = |d^2 \mathbf{R}/dt^2| = \omega^2 r\).
    • \(|\mathbf{a}| = (a_t^2 + a_n^2)^{1/2} = a_n = \kappa \nu^2\).
    • \(a_n = \omega^2 r\), and \(\kappa = a_n/ \nu^2 = \omega^2 r / \omega^2 r^2 = 1 /r\).

  • Example 2.17 The coordinate of a particle at time \(t\) are \[ x = \sin t - t \cos t, \quad y = \cos t + t \sin t, \quad z = t^2. \] Find the speed, the normal and tangential components of acceleration, and the curvature of the path, in terms of \(t\).

    Solution

    • \(\mathbf{R}(t) = (\sin t - t \cos t) \mathbf{i} + (\cos t + t \sin t) \mathbf{j} + t^2 \mathbf{k}\).
    • \(d \mathbf{R}/ dt = (t \sin t) \mathbf{i} + (t \cos t) \mathbf{j} + 2t \mathbf{k}\).
    • \(d^2 \mathbf{R}/dt^2 = (t \cos t + \sin t) \mathbf{i} + (- t \sin t + \cos t) \mathbf{j} + 2 \mathbf{k}\).
    • \(\nu = |d \mathbf{R}/dt| = (t^2 \sin^2 t + t^2 \cos^2 t + 4t^2)^{1/2} = \sqrt{5}t\).
    • \(a_t = d\nu/ dt = \sqrt{5}\).

    • By the Eq. \eqref{eq:2.42},

      \begin{align*} a_n & = (|\mathbf{a}|^2 - a_t^2)^{1/2}\\ & = [(t \cos t + \sin t)^2 + (-t \sin t + \cos t)^2 + 2^2 - 5]^{1/2} = t. \end{align*}
    • \(\kappa = a_n/ \nu^2 = t / (5t^2) = 1 /(5t)\).
  • Another formula to compute the curvature: \[ \kappa = \frac{| \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{a}|}{\nu^3}. \]

    • First of all,

      \begin{align*} \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{a} & = \nu \mathbf{T} \times (a_t \mathbf{T} + a_n \mathbf{N})\\ & = \nu a_n (\mathbf{T} \times \mathbf{N})\\ & = \kappa \nu^3 (\mathbf{T} \times \mathbf{N}). \end{align*}
    • Note that \(\mathbf{T}\) and \(\mathbf{N}\) are perpendicular to each other and they are unit vectors, so \[ \vert \mathbf{T} \times \mathbf{N}\vert = 1. \]
  • We call the unit vector \(\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{T}\times \mathbf{N}\) the binormal.
    • The triple \(\{\mathbf{T}, \mathbf{N}, \mathbf{B}\}\) forms a right-handed orthonormal system.
    • The torsion is defined by \[ \tau = \mathbf{B} \cdot \frac{d \mathbf{N}}{ds} \] which measures the rate at which the curve twists.
  • Note: Although many of the formulas we have learned involve derivatives with respect to arc length \(s\), one never actually needs to compute the natural parametrization \(\mathbf{R}(s)\), because of the chain rule: \[ \frac{d}{ds} = \frac{1}{|\mathbf{v}|}\frac{d}{dt}. \]

2.4 Planar Motion in Polar Coordinates

  • In this section, we consider the motion of a particle in the \(xy\) plane where the position of the particle is given in /polar coordinates, \(r\) and \(\theta\):

    \begin{align*} x & = r \cos \theta, \quad y = r \sin \theta\\ r & = (x^2 + y^2)^{1/2} \end{align*}

  • In order to work directly with polar coordinates, it is convenient to introduce unit vectors \(\mathbf{u}_r\) and \(\mathbf{u}_\theta\):

    \begin{align*} \mathbf{u}_r & = \cos \theta \mathbf{i} + \sin \theta \mathbf{j}\\ \mathbf{u}_\theta & = - \sin \theta \mathbf{i} + \cos \theta \mathbf{j}. \end{align*}

  • Accordingly,

    \begin{align*} \frac{d \mathbf{u}_r}{d \theta} & = \mathbf{u}_\theta,\\ \frac{d \mathbf{u}_\theta}{d\theta} & = - \mathbf{u}_r. \end{align*}
  • The position vector of a particle located at a point \((r, \theta)\) is \[ \mathbf{R} = r \mathbf{u}_r. \]

  • Then the velocity

    \begin{align*} \mathbf{v} & = \frac{d \mathbf{R}}{dt} = \frac{d r}{dt}\mathbf{u}_r + r \frac{d \mathbf{u}_r}{dt}\\ & = \frac{d r}{dt} \mathbf{u}_r + r \frac{d \mathbf{u}_r}{d\theta} \frac{d \theta}{dt}\\ & = \frac{d r}{dt} \mathbf{u}_r + r \frac{d \theta }{d t} \mathbf{u}_\theta. \end{align*}

    This expresses the velocity as the sum of a radial component, directed away from or toward the origin with magnitude \(|dr /dt|\), and a transverse component with magnitude \(|r d\theta/dt|\).

  • Example 2.18 A particle moves around the circle \(r = 2\) with angular velocity \(d \theta /dt = 5\) rad/sec. Find its speed.

    Solution Since \(r\) is a constant, \(dr/dt =0\). Hence, \[ \mathbf{v} = r \frac{d \theta}{dt} \mathbf{u}_\theta = 10 \mathbf{u}_\theta. \] Therefore, \(|\mathbf{v}| = 10\).
